# Signal Propagation

## An Algorithmic Philosophy

Signal Propagation explores the invisible architecture of live broadcast — the moment a stream goes live and its signal radiates outward, encountering other signals, interfering, amplifying, and creating emergent patterns of connection that no single broadcaster intended. The philosophy treats each particle as a carrier wave emanating from a source node, its trajectory shaped by layered Perlin noise fields that represent the turbulent topology of a network. Where signals converge, constructive interference creates bright nodes of attention — the algorithmic equivalent of thousands of viewers locking onto the same frequency. Where they diverge, silence. The beauty is in the propagation itself: the space between source and receiver, rendered visible.

The computational process begins with source nodes — points of origin that pulse with energy, each one a broadcast tower seeding waves into a shared medium. These nodes are not placed randomly but emerge from a seeded golden-angle spiral, ensuring organic but non-uniform distribution across the canvas. From each source, hundreds of signal particles are born with initial velocity vectors pointing radially outward, then immediately bent by a multi-octave noise field that represents atmospheric interference, network topology, and the chaos of competing signals. The particles carry phase information — a sinusoidal oscillation tied to their distance from origin — and their visual weight (opacity, width) is modulated by this phase. The result is concentric wave-fronts that warp, stretch, and fragment as they propagate through the noise field. This is a meticulously crafted system where every noise octave, every damping coefficient, every phase offset has been calibrated through painstaking iteration to produce wave patterns that feel physically plausible yet aesthetically transcendent.

Color in Signal Propagation is not decorative — it is informational. Each source node owns a hue from a curated palette, and its emitted particles inherit that hue with subtle variation. As particles from different sources cross paths, their colors blend additively in the trail buffer, creating interference bands of mixed color that reveal which signals dominate which regions of the canvas. High-velocity particles burn brighter (higher saturation, lower opacity for ethereal trails); slow particles approaching equilibrium fade toward the background, leaving ghost traces. The palette itself is constrained: warm ambers and cool cyans create a natural tension — fire and ice, signal and noise — with a single accent green that appears only at points of maximum constructive interference. Every color relationship is the product of deep computational expertise, refined through countless seed explorations until the palette achieves that rare quality where every hue feels inevitable.

Temporal evolution is central. The algorithm does not render a single frame — it accumulates. A translucent background wash each frame creates trail persistence, and over hundreds of frames the canvas develops a density map: bright, layered regions where many signals have passed, and dark voids where the noise field created dead zones. Particles have finite lifespans tied to their energy (initial velocity decays through drag), and when they die, new ones are born at the source nodes, ensuring the system breathes — expanding and contracting like a living network. The master-level implementation ensures that the system reaches a visual equilibrium around frame 400, where new births balance deaths and the composition stabilizes into its final form without ever truly stopping. Each seed produces a unique network topology, a unique interference pattern — the same algorithm, infinite expressions. This is the mark of a painstakingly optimized generative system built by someone at the absolute pinnacle of computational aesthetics.
